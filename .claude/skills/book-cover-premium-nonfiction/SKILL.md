# Book Cover — Premium Nonfiction

Use this skill when designing, reviewing, or iterating on a premium business nonfiction book cover, especially when the brief requires typographic restraint, subtle conceptual imagery, and strong thumbnail readability.

This skill is tuned for the contest cover:
- Title: Nature of Product
- Subtitle: How to Build Products So Valuable They Look Like Magic
- Author: Ivan Zamesin
- Category: premium business nonfiction
- Concept: a barely noticeable fractal or self-similar structure that reflects nested goals / Job Graph logic

## Core Goal

Design a cover that could sit naturally next to:
- Atomic Habits
- Thinking, Fast and Slow
- The Innovator's Dilemma
- Stripe Press titles

The intended feel is:
- calm
- intelligent
- premium
- restrained
- contemporary but not trendy
- confident without trying too hard

Typography should lead. The visual motif should support the typography, not compete with it.

## Non-Negotiables

- The title must remain the primary visual anchor.
- The subtitle must be readable but clearly secondary.
- The author name must support the hierarchy, not dominate it.
- The image or fractal layer must stay subtle and discoverable on second glance.
- The cover must remain legible and convincing at thumbnail size.
- Use no more than 2 font families.
- Prefer a tight palette: background + text + one restrained accent.
- Avoid anything that looks like generic AI-generated startup design.

## Strong Preferences

- Prefer serif-led typography for the main title.
- Prefer title case, not all caps, for the main title unless there is a very strong reason.
- Use generous spacing and visible calm.
- Let the composition feel editorial rather than promotional.
- If using a fractal or graph structure, it should feel atmospheric, embedded, and almost subliminal.
- If forced to choose between conceptual cleverness and thumbnail readability, choose readability.

## Avoid Completely

Do not suggest or introduce:
- brains
- rockets
- arrows going up
- gears
- light bulbs
- glowing nodes
- wireframe networks as a dominant motif
- neon gradients
- cyberpunk aesthetics
- glossy 3D blobs
- text shadows
- bevels
- metallic effects
- fake luxury tropes
- loud symmetry that makes the cover feel decorative instead of intelligent
- visual clutter used to “explain” the concept too literally

Do not make the fractal:
- centered as the hero object
- high contrast
- technically flashy for its own sake
- more important than the title

## How to Think

When reviewing or generating ideas, reason in this order:

1. Hierarchy
- Is the title clearly dominant?
- Is the subtitle placed and sized so it supports the title?
- Does the author line feel calm and appropriate?

2. Shelf Presence
- Would this look expensive and intentional next to serious nonfiction?
- Would it stand out through restraint rather than noise?

3. Thumbnail Performance
- Is the title still readable at very small size?
- Does the overall silhouette remain recognizable?
- Does the cover collapse into mush when scaled down?

4. Concept Subtlety
- Is the metaphor present without being literal?
- Does the fractal/self-similarity idea reward a second look?
- Is the concept being over-explained visually?

5. Typographic Quality
- Are line breaks elegant?
- Is spacing between title, subtitle, and author optically balanced?
- Does the title feel stable and confident?

6. Palette Discipline
- Is the palette restrained?
- Is the accent necessary?
- Would removing one color improve the result?

7. Anti-Slop Check
- Does this look like an AI template?
- Is the composition too centered, too balanced, too safe, or too decorated?
- Is there anything that feels default rather than authored?

## Output Style

When you respond:
- be concise
- be specific
- prefer exact changes over general taste advice
- preserve the concept unless asked to explore a new direction
- suggest the smallest effective change first
- avoid giving 20 options when 3 strong options are enough

When comparing directions:
- rank them from strongest to weakest
- explain why in plain language
- reject weak directions clearly
- do not soften criticism when something feels generic

## Review Rubric

Score each direction from 1 to 5 on:
- Premium feel
- Typographic hierarchy
- Thumbnail readability
- Concept subtlety
- Shelf presence
- Originality without gimmicks

Interpretation:
- 5 = competition-ready
- 4 = strong, needs polish
- 3 = promising but unresolved
- 2 = weak direction
- 1 = reject

If a direction scores below 4 in premium feel or thumbnail readability, do not recommend it as a finalist.

## Figma Review Mode

When a Figma frame or screenshot is provided:
- identify the current hierarchy first
- point out exactly what is too large, too small, too tight, too loud, or too vague
- suggest concrete changes in placement, scale, spacing, or contrast
- comment on whether the fractal layer is too visible or too decorative
- prioritize decisions that improve print-like authority

Good example response shape:
- “Reduce subtitle size by about 10–15%.”
- “Move the title block slightly higher to create a more deliberate lower field.”
- “Fade the background structure until it is almost not consciously noticed.”
- “The author name is competing with the subtitle; reduce contrast or size.”

## Code / SVG Mode

When asked to generate code-based cover elements:
- prefer SVG structures that remain editable
- keep algorithmic backgrounds subtle, low contrast, and typography-safe
- generate multiple density/contrast variants rather than one dramatic effect
- avoid perfect symmetry unless it strengthens the editorial feel
- treat the code-generated motif as support material, not the core of the cover

If creating fractal or self-similar motifs:
- prioritize elegance over mathematical showmanship
- use low visual frequency near key text areas
- preserve calm negative space around the title block
- prefer softness, rhythm, and structure over spectacle

## Exploration Rules

Default exploration set should be narrow.
Do not explode the search space unless explicitly asked.

Prefer:
- 3 strong directions
- 2 palette variants max per direction
- 2 type pairings max in one round

Avoid giant matrices of combinations unless the user explicitly wants exhaustive exploration.

## Final Check

Before calling a cover direction strong, verify:
- It still works without needing explanation.
- It still feels premium in grayscale.
- It still works in thumbnail.
- The fractal remains subordinate.
- The typography carries the cover.
- It does not look self-published.
- It does not look like generic AI output.

- “Rank these cover options and reject weak ones.”
- “Suggest the smallest changes needed to make this feel more premium.”
- “Generate a subtle SVG background motif that will not overpower typography.”
- “Check whether this cover still works at thumbnail size.”

## Short Reminder

For this project, taste means restraint.
If the idea is visible too fast, it is probably too loud.
If the image is more memorable than the title, the hierarchy is wrong.
If the cover feels like it is trying to impress, pull it back.
